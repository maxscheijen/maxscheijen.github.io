Real-time Effective Reproductive Number

Estimating in real-time the Dutch effective repoductive number

Using Bayesian statistics to estimate the real-time effective repoductive number allow us to predict future covid case count in the Netherlands.
analysis
covid
Author

Max Scheijen

Published

October 16, 2020

The effective reproduction number \(R_t\) is a way to quantify how quickly a virus spreads in a population. It estimates the number of people infected by a single person. \(R_t\) is an indicator of how fast a virus is spreading. This post displays the \(R_t\) for COVID-19 in the Netherlands.

The graph below displays a real-time estimate of the \(R_t\) in the Netherlands and its provinces. This estimate is based on the daily COVID-19 case counts provided by the RIVM (2021).

The controls allow you to select a province and compare it to the national average. The bands display the 90% confidence interval for the highlighted province. The smoothing helps with removing short-term fluctuations and highlighting longer-term trends or cycles by taking the average \(R_t\)/cases over a specified day range. The dashed gray lines are the non-selected provinces.


The graph below displays the daily COVID-19 case counts. It follows the same color scheme as the \(R_t\) graph. The case count for the Netherlands is an average over all the provinces. Therefore it doesn’t follow the absolute case count for the Netherlands.

The estimation of \(R_t\) is based on the smoothed daily case count. As suggest by Systrom et al.{% cite systrom2020_rtcovid %}, a gaussian filter is applied to the daily case count time-series. This reduces the noise of the stochastic nature of case counts, caused by late reporting or corrections. A window size of 7 days is arbitrarily chosen to smooth this stochastic process. This gives late-stage reporting, back-logs and corrections a week to catch up. The real-world infections are much less stochastic than the process of reporting, making smoothing necessary. The smoothing applied to the daily case count displayed in the figure below.

Later on, Systrom, Vladek, and Krieger (2020) developed a more complex Bayesian model that uses the positive test rate and MCMC sampling to estimate \(R_t\), as seen on rt.live. The positive test rate is a more useful measure because the number of raw positive tests is influenced by how many people are tested. The more you test, the more positive cases you will find. Therefore making test positive rate more informative. Furthermore, this simple model does not take into account the onset distribution or the incubation period of the virus. More plainly stated this is the distribution of the delay between infection and confirmed positive test. The more complex Bayesian model does take this into account.

Note: All credit for developing this model goes to Systrom, Vladek, and Krieger (2020). They did all the hard work. I simply applied their insights, research, and model to estimate the real-time \(R_t\) in the Netherlands. The visualization is heavily inspired by Fil (2021) graph about mobility data in France.
